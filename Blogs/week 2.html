Digital Media

Week 2

24 February 2025

Reflection

Moulthrop, S. (2003) “You Say You Want a Revolution? Hypertext and the Laws of Media (1991)” was written well before the internet took dominance but is much more relevant in this day and age. Stuart Moulthrop contrasts Theodor Holm Nelson’s vision of Xanadu with the notion of hyperreality to explore the tension between hypertext as a tool for empowerment and hypertext as a medium that blurs reality and simulation in ways that might be disorienting or even oppressive.

I believe that Nelson had an epic idea to create a non-linear knowledge network that would allow users to link, retrieve, and compare documents dynamically. Nelson imagined the user being able to interact with the information via bidirectional linking, where a document could be linked back to all the places it was referenced. Essentially, Nelson's goal was to make knowledge more dynamic.

Moulthrop, however, brings the concept of hyperreality to critique the potential dangers of hypertext. He raises some valid points: how hypertext—though promising—might actually collapse meaning rather than clarify it. If I were trying to navigate an infinite web of links, I might get disoriented, making it difficult to form a coherent understanding.

A great example of this in the modern internet is social media. There are so many rabbit holes one might fall into while trying to get a sense of truth. The truth nowadays has become more relative due to the many sources people can access. Moulthrop doesn't outright reject Nelson's vision, but he raises important critiques of hypertext—ones that feel incredibly relevant today.
