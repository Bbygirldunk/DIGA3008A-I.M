Building an Ethical Internet

27 May 2025

What Is an Ethical Internet?

An ethical internet, to me, is one that puts human dignity first. It's one where people feel safe, where privacy is respected, and where individuals have control over how their data is used and shared. At its core, an ethical internet must be built on the principles of safety, inclusivity, accountability, and transparency.

Safety and Harm Reduction

Online safety is about more than keeping people safe from bad content; it’s about making the online environment a place where individuals are not harassed, doxxed, or attacked due to who they are. Online spaces currently mirror real-world inequalities and even intensify them. Women, LGBTQ+ individuals, and marginalized groups are habitually harassed. An ethical internet would address this with rigorous content moderation and community standards that put harm reduction first—without suppressing valid discussion.

Privacy and Data Sovereignty

In our current digital ecosystem, users are treated less like individuals and more like data points. Each scroll, search, and click is tracked, saved, and sold to advertisers. This commodification happens with little genuine consent or knowledge. An ethical internet must give users full transparency and control over their data—not just in lengthy terms and conditions but in accessible, meaningful ways. This includes embracing data minimalism and making privacy the default, not the exception.

Digital Rights and Public Ownership

Protection also extends to digital rights: the right to speak, to organize, to be present online without being unfairly monitored or censored. As Stein (2008) points out, much of the internet, though considered public, is privately owned and controlled. This creates a paradox where speech is formally free but pragmatically limited. In an ethical internet, individuals wouldn’t rely on tech firms’ goodwill to exercise their rights—there would be structural guarantees like those that exist in physical public space.

Reclaiming the Virtual Sphere

Papacharissi (2020) describes the "virtual sphere" as a projection of the public sphere into digital space. While it holds promise for democratic engagement, commercialization and inequitable access too often limit that potential. An ethical internet would reclaim this promise, prioritizing civic engagement, critical debate, and equal access—not just efficiency or profit.

Ethics as Infrastructure

An ethical internet isn’t just a technical challenge—it’s a moral one. We need to ask whose voices are amplified, whose data is safeguarded, and who benefits from the systems we build. This responsibility lies not only with users but with developers, platforms, and governments. If we want the internet to be a space of learning, growth, and connection, ethics must be built in—not bolted on afterwards.
