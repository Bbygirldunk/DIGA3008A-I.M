The Geography of the Internet: Uneven Connections in a Connected World

Shane Greenstein, Chris Forman, and Avi Goldfarb (2018) challenge the popular belief that the Internet is a “borderless” technology. Their chapter in The Oxford Handbook of Economic Geography reveals that rather than flattening the world, the Internet reflects and amplifies existing geographic, economic, and institutional inequalities. This post explores their core arguments, methods, and insights while weaving in ideas from digital geography and media studies.

Article Objectives

The authors aim to:

  • Disprove the idea that the Internet removes geography as a relevant factor.
  • Show how digital infrastructures reshape—but don’t erase—spatial and economic patterns.
  • Bridge the gap between economic geography and information technology.

Methods and Sources

Instead of new data, the authors conduct a conceptual synthesis—reviewing empirical research, mainly U.S.-based firm-level economic data. While strong in its analytical reach, this approach lacks depth on global inequalities, especially from the Global South (see Graham, 2011).

Geography Matters

The Internet’s rollout is uneven and tied to local factors like urbanization, institutional support, and economic development. Stronger regions gain more from IT adoption, reinforcing spatial inequalities. This aligns with ideas from:

  • Saskia Sassen (2001): global cities concentrate digital power.
  • Doreen Massey (1994): “power-geometry”—control over networks is unevenly distributed.

The Internet Shapes Geography

While digital tools enable decentralization and global reach, benefits are uneven. “Micro multinationals” can thrive, but only with strong local infrastructure. This resonates with Mark Graham’s (2013) work on digital labor, showing that digital access alone doesn’t guarantee equity—platforms can replicate old hierarchies.

Contributions and Critiques

The chapter offers a compelling bidirectional model: geography shapes the Internet, and the Internet reshapes geography. But its narrow economic lens overlooks:

  • Cultural and experiential geographies of digital space.
  • Race, gender, class, and postcolonial dynamics.
  • Critical media and anthropological insights into infrastructural politics.

Future Directions

Future studies should include qualitative and interdisciplinary approaches—ethnographies of digital life, platform politics, and environmental impacts (e.g., data centers, e-waste). These can reveal the lived realities behind the economic data and help guide more just and inclusive digital development.

Conclusion

Greenstein, Forman, and Goldfarb’s work reminds us that the Internet is not a neutral force. It's built on uneven terrain, and it often deepens existing divides. If we want a more equitable digital future, we must understand and address the spatial politics at its core.
