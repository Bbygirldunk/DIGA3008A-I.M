Making a Feminist Internet

20 May 2025

Magenya (2020) — Rethinking Digital Space

Reading Salome Magenya’s Making a Feminist Internet in Africa made me stop and think about how I use the internet. I’ve always thought of it as this open, global space where anyone can express themselves or connect with others. But Magenya challenges that idea completely. She shows how the internet, like every other system—is shaped by power, patriarchy, capitalism, and colonial histories. That stuck with me.

Beyond Access: Rebuilding the Web

What I found most powerful was her point that African feminists don’t just need access to the internet, they need to be part of rethinking and rebuilding it. She shows how the internet often erases African women’s voices, even while pretending to be open and democratic. That made me think of postcolonial feminism—the idea that we can’t treat all women’s experiences as the same, and that Western frameworks often misrepresent or erase the realities of women in the Global South.

Agency and Responsibility

Magenya’s argument that we’re not just using the internet, but actively making it, really changed how I see my own role online. My platform choices, the voices I uplift, what I share, all of that shapes digital space. That realization gave me a sense of agency, but also a deeper responsibility to be intentional about what I’m participating in.

Offline Violence, Online Echoes

She also emphasizes that online spaces reflect offline realities. The same violence and silencing African women face offline follow them online. This challenges the idea that the internet automatically offers equality or freedom. It doesn’t, unless it’s *intentionally* designed that way.

Final Thoughts

This reading made it clear: technology is not neutral. Magenya’s call for African feminists to be central in shaping the internet isn’t just about inclusion—it’s about transformation. It left me asking: what kind of internet am I helping to build? Who is it for? And who might it still leave behind?
